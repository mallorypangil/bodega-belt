01 The dependency gap

Harlem has 3.5× more bodegas per 1,000 residents than the Upper East Side. At first glance this looks like abundance — more stores, better access. But the number tells the opposite story.

"The bodega density isn't a sign of food wealth. It's a sign of food need."

UES residents have alternatives: large supermarkets, specialty grocers, meal kit delivery, and — critically — cars. Only about 19% of Harlem households own a vehicle, compared to roughly 35% on the UES. Without a car, a supermarket a mile away is effectively inaccessible for a weekly grocery run. The bodega fills that gap.

This is why bodega density and food insecurity can correlate positively. More bodegas doesn't mean more food security — it means residents are more reliant on small-format retail that typically charges higher prices and carries less fresh produce.

02 What the data does and doesn't show

This project maps NY State-licensed small grocery stores (establishment types A and AC, under 3,000 sq ft) in Harlem ZIPs 10026–10039 and UES ZIPs 10021–10128. The 10-minute walk radius (800m) is a standard food access benchmark used by the USDA.

What the map shows well: spatial distribution of small-format food retail, and the density contrast between two neighborhoods with very different income levels.

What it doesn't show: supermarkets, bodegas without state licenses, produce carts and street vendors (significant in Harlem), food pantries, or the quality and price of food inside each store. A bodega that stocks mostly chips and cigarettes counts the same as one with a full deli and produce section.

Methodology notes

Data source: NY State Dept. of Agriculture and Markets, Retail Food Store Licensing dataset (data.ny.gov/9a8c-vfzj), filtered to active licenses as of early 2024.

Store filter: Establishment type A (grocery) or AC (grocery + meat/deli), square footage ≤ 3,000 sq ft or unreported. This excludes large supermarkets (typically type B or above) while capturing the bodega/corner store category.

Coverage estimate: Computed by sampling a 25×25 grid of points across the bounding box and measuring what percentage fall within 800m of at least one store. This is a geometric approximation — it doesn't account for actual street routing or building obstacles.

Population figures: ACS 2022 5-year estimates. Harlem NTA population ~110,000; UES NTAs ~220,000.

04 The broader picture

Harlem's food landscape has changed significantly since the 1990s. Gentrification has brought new restaurants, cafés, and specialty food stores — but these serve new residents, not necessarily long-term community members. Longtime residents report that stores they relied on have been replaced by businesses priced out of their reach.

The USDA defines a food desert as a low-income census tract where a substantial number of residents have low access to a supermarket or large grocery store. Parts of Harlem meet this definition. But the bodega-as-infrastructure story is more nuanced: high bodega density can actually mask food desert status in USDA metrics, since the stores technically exist — even if they don't offer the nutrition, variety, or pricing of a full supermarket.

The 24% of Harlem's bounding area that falls outside a 10-minute walk of any bodega is also unequally distributed. It clusters in the northern reaches of the neighborhood, around 145th Street and above — areas with lower density, fewer transit options, and higher proportions of elderly residents for whom a long walk to buy groceries is not just inconvenient but genuinely limiting.

"A map of bodega locations is also a map of who can't afford to shop elsewhere."
